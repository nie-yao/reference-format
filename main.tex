\begin{abstract}
This paper addresses the relay allocation problem for distributed state estimation in wireless sensor networks. Each sensor operates a state estimator to generate local predictions, which are then transmitted to its neighboring sensors over a shared network with a limited number of relays. When the sensor receives predictions from neighbors, it employs the Kalman-like consensus filter to conduct state estimate while accounting for channel uncertainties. Given the constrained availability of relays, only a subset of sensors can utilize them at any given time. To enhance the overall estimation performance, an allocation mechanism is implemented to dynamically allocate relays to selected sensors. This allocation problem is formulated as a Markov decision process, and a Deep Q-Network (DQN) algorithm is developed to solve it efficiently in a scalable and model-free manner. The proposed approach is capable of handling large-scale sensor networks and adapting to varying network conditions. Finally, an illustrative example is provided to demonstrate that the proposed approach achieves superior estimation performance and more effective relay utilization.

\section{Introduction}
Cyber-physical systems (CPSs), which integrate communication networks with physical infrastructures such as sensors, controllers, and actuators \cite{xing2023optimal}, have garnered substantial attention due to their transformative role in bridging computational algorithms and physical processes. CPSs empower advanced applications such as autonomous vehicles, healthcare robotics, industrial automation, precision agriculture and so on, which enabling real-time monitoring, adaptive decision-making, and dynamic optimization \cite{schutze2018sensors}. The analysis and synthesis of dynamical systems over CPSs encompass, but are not limited to, control \cite{ji2023adaptive, xi2025expert, priya2025dynamic}, synchronization \cite{zhang2024synchronization, hannon2021distributed}, and filtering \cite{jin2023distributed, li2024improving, xing2022dynamic, zheng2022distributed}.

In particular, the distributed filtering problem has been extensively studied within the framework of networked systems due to its broad applicability across various domains. As a particularly suitable technique for dynamical systems (over CPSs), distributed filtering utilizes the collaborative capabilities of sensor nodes to process data efficiently while reducing communication overhead. Distributed filtering is widely employed in critical applications demanding accurate state estimation in real time, including vehicle tracking, indoor localization, environmental monitoring, orbit determination, and navigation. Its primary objective lies in estimating unknown system states by leveraging local sensor measurements and collaborative interactions among neighboring nodes within specified network topologies, thereby ensuring both robustness and scalability. For comprehensive discussions and foundational methodologies, see \cite{zhou2024finite, song2024robust, li2024distributed, fu2024distributed, feng2022enhanced} and associated references.

The widely employed distributed filtering approaches can be broadly categorized into distributed extended Kalman filtering \cite{shen2023distributed, chen2022information}, distributed particle filtering \cite{xia2020direct, liu2023mesh}, distributed cubature filtering \cite{zhou2024robust}, distributed $H\infty$ filtering \cite{ding2015distributed}, and other variants, each tailored to handle distinct system complexities and operational constraints. Among these methodologies, distributed Kalman-type filtering algorithms maintain their core status in both academia and industry owing to their theoretical rigor, engineering practicality, and continuous evolvability, continuing to be a prominent research focus, necessitating continuous theoretical refinements, practical enhancements, and innovative implementations to advance their performance in diverse applications. The Distributed Kalman Filter (DKF) leverages localized Kalman filters to establish consensus among all the sensor estimates \cite{olfati2007distributed,olfati2005distributed}. Each sensor aggregates measurements shared by the neighboring nodes, then integrate these data to refine its local estimate. Through this collaborative process, the sensor network achieves a collective observation of the system state. The introduction of such distributed estimation paradigms has significantly enhanced network scalability while substantially lowering deployment costs.
%广泛使用的分布式滤波方法可以被大致分为。。。在这些方法中，分布式卡尔曼滤波算法因其理论深度、工程友好性及持续演进能力，在学术界与工业界保持核心地位，需要不断的理论改进、实践增强和创新实现，以提高其在各种应用中的性能。

Current literature almost operates under the widely accepted assumption of sensors possessing unlimited signal transmission range. However, this idealized assumption is not always correct because of practical engineering constraints arising from finite transmission capacity and channel fading phenomena \cite{li2022event, liu2021distributed}. Such limitations degrade estimation precision and control efficacy, particularly in energy-constrained systems or extensive network deployments. An effective resolution to overcome these transmission challenges is to implement single or multi-tier relay architectures between sensor node pairs \cite{ behbahani2012linear, lloyd2006relay, leong2015kalman, khajehnouri2007distributed, tan2020robust, sahebdel2011relay}. Relay architectures are typically categorized by their signal processing strategies, such as amplify-and-forward (AF), decode-and-forward, compress-and-forward, compute-and-forward, and filter-and-forward schemes \cite{liu2022auxiliary},\cite{lloyd2006relay},\cite{pang2021false},\cite{qu2022probabilistic},\cite{quevedo2012state}. Among these, the AF relay, which amplifies and rebroadcasts received signals without decoding, has shown particular promise in improving fusion estimation performance. By enhancing signal-to-noise ratios at the estimator, AF relays enable higher-quality state reconstruction \cite{han2023recursive}, making them a compelling focus for CPS applications.

Critically, in real-world engineering, communication resources (e.g., spectrum availability and transmission power) are intrinsically constrained. These limitations render ubiquitous relay access for all sensors in large-scale CPSs infeasible. This will induce data collisions and degraded estimation accuracy \cite{cheng2021observer}. To counteract this, relay allocation strategies have been developed to dynamically allocate relays to sensors, maximizing diversity gain while mitigating contention issues in cooperative networks \cite{s2020review}. Notwithstanding that relay assignment has been rigorously investigated for wireless architectures \cite{lyu2023source, shi2008optimal, wang2016relay, xie2013optimal, zhang2011relay}, its integration with distributed filtering in CPSs persists as an underexplored domain, despite its crucial role in practical industrial scenarios. For example, \cite{han2023recursive} investigates distributed filtering in discrete time-varying systems employing amplify-and-forward relays within binary sensor networks. However, the relay resource constraint has not been considered in this paper, so it does not conduct relay scheduling.

Except for relay scheduling complexities, the scalability challenge in CPSs is amplified by the combinatorial explosion of system components. The exponential growth of sensors and relays generates high-dimensional Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) characterized by intractably prohibitive state and action spaces. Traditional reinforcement learning (RL) approaches, exemplified by Q-learning, face fundamental limitations due to the dimensionality curse inherent in tabular value function representations \cite{bertsekas2012dynamic}. Deep reinforcement learning (DRL), particularly Deep Q-Networks (DQN) \cite{guo2023reinforcement, mnih2013playing, mnih2015human}, addresses this limitation by employing neural networks as function approximators to enable scalable and model-free solutions for complex scheduling tasks\cite{sutton1998reinforcement}. While relay assignment strategies offer a means to optimize sensor-to-relay allocation, recent studies \cite{alali2024deep, jung2023optimized, leong2020deep} have applied DRL to sensor scheduling without considering two critical aspects: the role of relays in enhancing transmission reliability and the integration of distributed wireless sensor network for state estimation.

The above challenges of relay resource constraints in CPSs motivate the development of a dynamic relay allocation algorithm suitable for distributed state estimation, in which only partial sensor subsets can access relays at each time step due to limited availability. Our objective focuses on minimizing the sum of estimation error covariances of each sensor by optimally selecting sensors for relay-assisted transmission. It is assumed that intelligent sensors, equipped with embedded Kalman-like filters, can compute local state estimates and transmit them to neighbors. The core problem is cast as an MDP, where states are defined by the estimation error covariance of each sensor. The core contributions of this work can be summarized as follows: (1) Under the established comprehensive model considering the effects of random fading channel and relays, an optimal distributed state estimation algorithm over switching topology caused by relay allocation is derived, which uses the Kalman consensus filtering logic in conjunction with weighted directed graphs; (2)The large-scale relay allocation problem for distributed state estimation is formulated as a high-dimensional MDP, which is to minimize the average estimation error covariance over the infinite time horizon.
(3) The DQN algorithm is designed to solve the MDP to seek optimal policies through a scalable and model-free approach.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. The system model, transmission model, and scheduling model are provided in Section \ref{sec:formulation}. Then, we present the development of the proposed distributed state estimation algorithm over switching topologies in Section \ref{sec:algorithm}. The relay allocation problem and the corresponding MDP are described in Section \ref{sec:DQN}, along with the deep reinforcement learning approach for allocation. Numerical studies are conducted in Section \ref{sec:experiment} to demonstrate the performance of the proposed algorithm. Finally, we conclude the paper and discuss some future work in Section \ref{sec:conclusion}.

\section{Problem Formulation} \label{sec:formulation}
\subsection{Sensor Network with Relays}
The communication topology of a sensor network considered in this paper is expressed as a weighted directed graph $\mathcal{G}=(\mathcal{V},\mathcal{E})$(See Fig.\ref{fig1}). The set of vertices $\mathcal{V} \triangleq \mathcal{V}_s\cup\mathcal{V}_r$, where $\mathcal{V}_s\triangleq\{s_1,s_2,\cdots,s_N\}$ and $\mathcal{V}_r\triangleq\{r_1,r_2,\cdots,r_M\}$ denote the sets of sensors and the relays, respectively. $s_i(i=1,2,\cdots,N)$ and $r_d(d=1,2,\cdots,M)$ denote the $i$-th sensor and the $d$-th relay, respectively.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{fig/topology.eps}
    \caption{The weighted directed topology with relays.}
    \label{fig1}
\end{figure}

The set of edges is denoted as $\mathcal{E}\subseteq\mathcal{V}\times\mathcal{V}$, where an edge of $\mathcal{G}$ is denoted by the ordered pair of two vertices. $s_j$ is called a neighbor of $s_i$ if $(s_i,s_j)\in \mathcal{E}$ (i.e., $s_j$ can directly send information to $s_i$) or $\{(s_i,r_d),(r_d,s_j)\}\subset\mathcal{E}$(i.e., $s_j$ can send information to $s_i$ via a relay $r_d$). For $s_i$, the sets of direct and indirect neighbors are, respectively, designated as $\mathcal{N}_{i1}\triangleq\{s_j|(s_i,s_j)\in\mathcal{E},s_j\neq s_i\}$ and $\mathcal{N}_{i2}\triangleq\{s_j|\{(s_i,r_d),(r_d,s_j)\}\subset\mathcal{E},s_j\neq s_i\}$. Moreover, the set of neighbors of $s_i$ is expressed as $\mathcal{N}_i\triangleq\mathcal{N}_{i1}\cup\mathcal{N}_{i2}$. For simplicity, we assume $\mathcal{N}_{i1}\cap\mathcal{N}_{i2}=\emptyset$, where $\emptyset$ is the empty set; if $s_j\in \mathcal{N}_{i2}$, then there exists only one relay to achieve the communications from $s_j$ to $s_i$.

\subsection{System Model}
A wireless sensor network consisting of N sensor nodes and M relays is considered in this paper, such as the one depicted in \ref{fig1}. The target dynamical system to be estimated is modeled as a linear time-varying discrete-time system:
%参考Analysis of the Compressed Distributed Kalman Filter over Markovian Switching Topology
\begin{equation}\label{target}
\begin{split}
        x_{k+1} = A x_k + w_k 
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ is the state vector,  $w_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ is the system noise. The initial state $x_{0}$ is a zero-mean Gaussian random vector with covariance $\Sigma_{0}\geq0$. The observation model of each sensor is
\begin{equation}\label{measurement}
\begin{split}
     y^{i}_{k}= C_{k}^{i} x_k + v^{i}_{k} \text{,}~ i = 1, 2, \dots, N
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $y^{i}_{k} \in \mathbb{R}^{m_i}$ is the measurement made by sensor $s_{i}$ at time $k$; $C_{k}^{i}$ is a known time-varying matrix with appropriate dimension and $v^{i}_{k} \in \mathbb{R}^{m_i}$ is the measurement noise. $w_k$ and $v^{i}_{k}$ both are independent zero-mean Gaussian noises with covariance matrix $Q > 0$ and $R_{i}> 0$. Given the history of measurements made by sensor $s_{i}$, $Y^{i}_{k}\triangleq\{y^{i}_{0},y^{i}_{1},\cdots,y^{i}_{k}\}$, we define:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\hat{x}^{i}_{k}&=\mathbb{E}[x(k)|Y^{i}_{k}]\\
\bar{x}^{i}_{k}&=\mathbb{E}[x(k)|Y^{i}_{k-1}]
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{x}^{i}_{k}$ and $\bar{x}^{i}_{k}$ are referred to as the state estimation and prediction of the target, respectively. Every sensor can observe the target, and send its prediction to its neighbors through a relay or not.

\subsubsection{Transmission to Direct Neighbors}
Sensor $s_j\in \mathcal{N}_{i1}$ directly sends its estimates $\hat{x}_{i,k}$ to sensor $s_i$ through a fading channel. Let $\hat{x}_{k}^{ij}$ be the signal received by sensor $s_i$. Taking the fading effect into consideration, we can model $\hat{x}_{k}^{ij}$ as follows:
% \begin{equation} \label{xij}
% \hat{x}_{ij,k}=F_{ij,k}(K_{ij,k}\hat{x}_{j,k}+\tilde{\vartheta}_{ij,k})
% \end{equation}
\begin{equation} \label{xij}
\bar{x}_{k}^{ij}=K_{k}^{ij}\bar{x}_{k}^{j}+\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{ij}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{ij}$ denotes the Gaussian noise in the sensor-to-sensor channel with $\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{ij}\}=0$ and $\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{ij}\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{ij\top}\}=\tilde{Q}_{k}^{ij}>0$. The channel gain matrix is defined as $K_{k}^{ij}\triangleq \diag\{K_{k}^{ij}(1),...,K_{k}^{ij}(n)\}$, where $K_{k}^{ij}(l)\in \mathbb{R}$ is a random variable with $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{ij}(l)\}= 1$, and therefore one has $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{ij}\}= I$.
Let $\tilde{K}_{k}^{ij}\triangleq K_{k}^{ij}-I$ with the $l$th diagonal element denoted by $\tilde{K}_{k}^{ij}(l)$, and define
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{ij}\triangleq\left[\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{K}_{k}^{ij}(m)\tilde{K}_{k}^{ij}(l)\}\right]_{n\times n}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Transmission to Indirect Neighbors}
Sensor $s_j$ first broadcasts $\hat{x}_{k}^{j}$ towards the relay $r_d$ through a fading channel. The estimate received by the relay is denoted as $\hat{x}_{dj,k}$ which is described as follows

% \end{equation}
\begin{equation} \label{xidj1}
\bar{x}_{k}^{dj}=K_{k}^{dj}\bar{x}_{k}^{j}+\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{dj}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{dj}$ is the Gaussian noise in the sensor-to-relay channel with $\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{dj}\}=0$ and $\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{dj}\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{dj\top}\}=\tilde{Q}_{k}^{dj}>0$, and $K_{k}^{dj}$ is the channel gain matrix. Moreover, $K_{k}^{dj}\triangleq \diag\{K_{k}^{dj}(1),...,K_{k}^{dj}(n)\}$, where $K_{k}^{dj}(l)\in \mathbb{R}$ is a random variable with $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{dj}(l)\}= 1$ and therefore one has $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{dj}\}= I$.
Let $\tilde{K}_{k}^{dj} \triangleq K_{k}^{dj}-I$ with the $l$th diagonal element denoted
by $\tilde{K}_{k}^{dj}(l)$ and define
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{dj}\triangleq\left[\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{K}_{k}^{dj}(m)\tilde{K}_{k}^{dj}(l)\}\right]_{n\times n}
\end{equation}
Then, relay $r_d$ rebroadcasts the signal vector $\bar{x}_{k}^{dj}$ to sensor $s_i$ through a fading channel. The signal received by sensor $s_i$ is represented as follows:
\begin{equation} \label{xidj3}
\bar{x}_{k}^{id_j}=K_{k}^{id_j}\Vec{x}_{k}^{dj}+\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{id_j}
\end{equation}
where $K_{k}^{id_j}$ is the channel gain matrix and $\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{id_j}$ denotes the Gaussian noise in the relay-to-sensor with $\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{id_j}\}= 0$ and $\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{id_j}\tilde{\vartheta}_{k}^{id_j\top}\} =\tilde{Q}_{k}^{id_j}$. Here, $K_{k}^{id_j} \triangleq \diag\{K_{k}^{id_j}(1),K_{k}^{id_j}(2),...,K_{k}^{id_j}(n)\}$, where $K_{k}^{id_j}(l)\in \mathbb{R}$ is a random variable with $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{id_j}(l)\}=1$. Furthermore, let $\tilde{K}_{k}^{id_j} \triangleq K_{k}^{id_j}-I$ with the $l$th diagonal element denoted by $\tilde{K}_{k}^{id_j}(l)$ and define
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{id_j}\triangleq\left[\mathbb{E}\{\tilde{K}_{k}^{id_j}(m)\tilde{K}_{k}^{id_j}(l)\}\right]_{n\times n}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Scheduling Model}
The sensors transmit their state predictions $\bar{x}_{k}^{i}$ to their neighbors via a relay or not over a shared wireless network with $M$ relays, which aims to estimate the internal state $x_{k}$ of the target plant. In typical applications, the number of relays is significantly less than the number of sensors (i.e., $M<<N$) due to resource constraints. Consequently, at most $M$ of the $N$ sensors can transmit with relays at any given time. At each time step $k$, $M$ relays will be allocated to the corresponding sensors selected by the DQN algorithm, which aims to achieve the best distributed state estimation performance. Noting that each relay may be assigned to a same sensor, due to the instances in which a sensor may have two or more neighbors. Define the decision variables $a_{d,k}$ for $d=1,...,M$ that satisfy $a_{d,k}\triangleq\{i,j\}(j\in \mathcal{N}_{k}^i)$ when the sensor $s_i$ is scheduled to transmit its prediction to its neighbor $s_j$ through relay $r_d$ at time $k$. 

Before proceeding further, let us introduce the following lemmas which will be used later.

\begin{lemma}(\cite{Topic in matrix analysis}~) \label{hada}
Let $\mathcal{X}=[x_{ij}]_{n\times n}$ be a real-valued matrix and $\mathcal{Y}=
\operatorname{diag}\{y_1,y_2,...,y_n\}$ be a diagonal random matrix. Then, we have
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}\{\mathcal{Y}\mathcal{X}\mathcal{Y}^\top \}=\begin{bmatrix}\mathbb{E}\{y_1^2\}&\mathbb{E}\{y_1y_2\}&...&\mathbb{E}\{y_1y_n\}\\\mathbb{E}\{y_2y_1\}&\mathbb{E}\{y_2^2\}&...&\mathbb{E}\{y_2y_n\}\\\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\\mathbb{E}\{y_ny_1\}&\mathbb{E}\{y_ny_2\}&...&\mathbb{E}\{y_n^2\}\end{bmatrix}\circ\mathcal{X} \text{,}
\end{align*}
where the symbol $\circ$ represents the Hadamard product defined as $[X\circ Y]_{ij}=x_{ij}y_{ij}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}(\cite{chen2021event}~)\label{derivation}
Let \( G \), \( H \), \( F \) and \( D \) be matrices with appropriate dimensions. Then, we have
\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial \tr(GHF)}{\partial H} &= G^\top F^\top, \\
\frac{\partial \tr(GHF)}{\partial H^\top} &= FG, \\
\frac{\partial \tr((GHF)D(GHF)^\top)}{\partial H} &= 2G^\top GHFDF^\top.
\end{align*}
\end{lemma}

\section{Algorithm Development} \label{sec:algorithm}

For sensor $s_{i}$, in addition to maintaining its own local prediction, it also may receive predictions from its direct or indirect neighbors. These predictions are subject to channel fading effects and relay interference during wireless transmission. Consequently, there are three corresponding distinct prediction errors:
%(我们在传感器网络中传输预测值)对于传感器节点i来说，除了具有自身本地的预测值之外，还有可能会收到来自直接邻居或间接邻居的预测值，这些预测值进行传输时会受到衰落信道以及中继的影响。因此，相应地，一共有如下三种情况的预测误差：

\underline{Case 1: Local prediction error}

Denote the prediction error of sensor $s_{i}$ itself as $\bar{\eta}_{k}^{i}\triangleq\bar{x}_{k}^{i}-x_{k}$. Based on \eqref{target} and \eqref{prediction}, we have
\begin{equation*}
\bar{\eta}_{k}^{i}=A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{i}-w_{k-1}
\end{equation*}

\underline{Case 2: Prediction error from the direct neighbor}

Denote the prediction error under this circumstance as $\bar{\eta}_{k}^{ij}\triangleq\bar{x}_{k}^{ij}-x_{k}$, which reflects the influence of fading channel (from sensor $s_{j}$ to sensor $s_{i}$) on the transmission signal $\bar{x}_{k}^{j}$. Based on \eqref{target}, \eqref{prediction} and \eqref{xij}, we have
%它表明了/显示了衰落信道对于传输信号x_j的影响
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\bar{\eta}_{k}^{ij}&=(K_{k}^{ij}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{j}+(K_{k}^{ij}-I)Ax_{k-1 }\\
&\quad+A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{j}+\tilde{v}_{k}^{ij}-w_{k-1}
\end{split}
\end{equation*}

\underline{Case 3: Prediction error from the indirect neighbor}

Denote the prediction error under this circumstance as $\bar{\eta}_{k}^{id_j}\triangleq\bar{x}_{k}^{id_j}-x_{k}$, which reflects the influence of fading channels (from sensor $s_j$ to relay $r_d$ and from relay $r_d$ to sensor $s_{i}$) and the relay $r_d$ on the transmission signal $\bar{x}_{k}^{j}$. Based on \eqref{target}, \eqref{prediction}, \eqref{xidj1} and \eqref{xidj3}, we have
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\bar{\eta}_{k}^{id_{j}}&=(K_{k}^{id_j}K_{k}^{dj}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{j}+K_{k}^{id_j}\tilde{v}_{k}^{dj}+\tilde{v}_{k}^{id_j}\\
&\quad+A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{j}+(K_{k}^{id_j}K_{k}^{dj}-I)Ax_{k-1}-w_{k-1})
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Then, subtracting $x_{k}$ from both sides of \eqref{heart} for sensor $s_{i}$, the estimation error $\hat{\eta}_{k}^{i}=\hat{x}_{k}^{i}-x_{k}$ is obtained
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\hat{\eta}_{k}^{i}&=(I-K_{k}^{i}C_{k}^{i})\bar{\eta}_{k}^{i}+\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_{k}^{i1}}H_{k}^{i,j}(\bar{\eta}_{k}^{i}-\bar{\eta}_{k}^{ij})\\
\quad&+\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_{k}^{i2}}G_{k}^{i,j}(\bar{\eta}_{k}^{i}-\bar{\eta}_{k}^{id_j})+K_{k}^{i}v_{k}^{i}\text{.}\\
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
The cross covariance matrices of the prediction and estimation errors are represented as:
\begin{equation}\label{covariancedef}
\begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0pt}}lr@{\hspace{0pt}}l}
\vspace{0.5em}
P_{k}^{m,n}&=\mathbb{E}[\bar{\eta}_{k}^{m}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{n\top}]\text{,} 
&P_{k}^{pm,qn}&=\mathbb{E}[\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pm}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{qn\top}]\text{,}\\
\vspace{0.5em}
P_{k}^{pd_m,qd_n}&=\mathbb{E}[\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pd_m}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{qd_n\top}]\text{,}
&P_{k}^{m,pn}&=\mathbb{E}[\bar{\eta}_{k}^{m}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pn\top}]\text{,}\\
\vspace{0.5em}
P_{k}^{m,pd_n}&=\mathbb{E}[\bar{\eta}_{k}^{m}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pd_n\top}]\text{,}
&P_{k}^{pm,qd_n}&=\mathbb{E}[\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pm}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{qd_n\top}]\text{,}\\
\vspace{0.5em}
M_{k}^{m,n}&=\mathbb{E}[\hat{\eta}_{k}^{m}\hat{\eta}_{k}^{n\top}]\text{.}&&
\end{array}   
\end{equation}

Then, the following lemmas can be stated.
\begin{lemma}\label{perror}
Prediction error covariances under different circumstances are calculated as follows:
%can be determined as
\begin{align*}
&P_{k}^{m,n} =\left\{\begin{array}{cl}%i,j→m,n

\end{array}\right.\\

\end{array}\right.\\
&P^{pd_m,qd_n}_{k} =\left\{\begin{array}{cl}
P_{k}^{pd_m}, &\text{if $m=n,p=q$}\\
AM_{k-1}^{m,n}A^\top+Q_{k-1}, &\text{otherwise}
\end{array}\right.\\
&P^{m,pn}_{k}=P^{m,pd_n}_{k}=P^{pm,qd_n}_{k}=AM_{k-1}^{m,n}A^\top+Q_{k-1}
\end{align*}
%和p有关系嘛？表示两个东西相互之间下标中有逗号，没有逗号的下标要么是表示一段路段，要么是表示两个相同的东西之间表达上进行的一个省略
where
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
P_{k}^{m}&=AM_{k-1}^{m}A^\top+Q_{k-1}\\
P_{k}^{pm}&=\mathscr{K}_{k}^{pm}\circ (AM_{k-1}^{m}A^\top)\\
&\quad+\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pm}\circ(A\Sigma_{k-1}A^\top)+\tilde{Q}_{k}^{pm}+Q_{k-1}\\
P_{k}^{pd_m}&=\mathscr{K}_{k}^{pd_m}\circ\mathscr{K}_{k}^{dm}\circ(A(M_{k-1}^{m}+\Sigma_{k-1})A^\top)\\
&\quad-A\Sigma_{k-1}A^\top+\mathscr{K}_{k}^{pd_m}\circ\tilde{Q}_{k}^{dm}+\tilde{Q}_{k}^{pd_m}+Q_{k-1}\\
\mathscr{K}_{k}^{pm}&=\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pm}+\mathbf{1}\\
\mathscr{K}_{k}^{pd_m}&=\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pd_m}+\mathbf{1}\\
\mathscr{K}_{k}^{dm}&=\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{dm}+\mathbf{1}\\
\Sigma_{k}&=A\Sigma_{k-1}A^T+Q.
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
For the sake of brevity, we only prove the most complicated circumstance $P^{pd_m,qd_n}_{k}$. When $m\neq n$ and $p\neq q$, namely $P_{k}^{pd_m,q
d_n}$, we have
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pd_m}&=(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}+K_{k}^{pd_m}\tilde{v}_{k}^{dm}+\tilde{v}_{k}^{pd_m}\\
&\quad+A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}+(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)Ax_{k-1}-w_{k-1})\\
\bar{\eta}_{k}^{qd_n}&=(K_{k}^{qd_n}K_{k}^{dn}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{n}+K_{k}^{qd_n}\tilde{v}_{k}^{dn}+\tilde{v}_{k}^{qd_n}\\
&\quad+A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{n}+(K_{k}^{qd_n}K_{k}^{dn}-I)Ax_{k-1}-w_{k-1})
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Noting that $K_{k}^{pd_m}$, $K_{k}^{dm}$, $K_{k}^{qd_n}$, $K_{k}^{dn}$, $\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{n\top}$ are uncorrelated to each other, and the values of $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}\}$, $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}\}$, $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}\}$, $\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}\}$ are all equal to $I$, the following is obtained:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
&\mathbb{E}\{((K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m})\\
&\quad\times((K_{k}^{qd_n}K_{k}^{dn}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{n})^\top\}\\
%&=\mathbb{E}\{((K_{pd_m,k}K_{dm,k}-I)\}\mathbb{E}\{A\hat{\eta}_{m,k-1}\hat{\eta}_{n,k-1}^\top A^\top\}\\
&\quad\times\mathbb{E}\{((K_{k}^{qd_n}K_{k}^{dn}-I)\}\\
&=(\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}\}\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{dm}\}-I)A\mathbb{E}\{\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{n\top} \}A^\top\\
&\quad\times(\mathbb{E}\{K_{qd_n,k}\}\mathbb{E}\{K_{dn,k}\}-I)\\
&=0
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
From the above equality and the fact that all the noise terms are mutually independent, it follows naturally that
\begin{equation*}
P^{pd_m,qd_n}_{k}=AM_{k-1}^{m,n}A^\top+Q_{k-1}~(m\neq n~ and~p\neq q )
\end{equation*}
The case of $m\neq n$ or $p\neq q$ is similar with the case of $m\neq n$ and $p\neq q$, and identical results can ultimately be derived for both cases. Now, we are in a position to prove the case of $m=n$ and $p=q$. To establish the expression for $P_{k}^{pd_m}$, the definition of it as $P_{k}^{pd_m}=\mathbb{E}\{\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pd_m}\bar{\eta}_{k}^{pd_m\top}\}$ is utilized. In view of the total expectation formula, the following expression can be calculated as:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
&\mathbb{E}\{(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m\top} A^\top(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)^\top\}\\
&=\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I\}A\mathbb{E}\{\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m\top}\}\\ 
&\quad\times A^\top\mathbb{E}\{(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)^\top\} .
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Given the definition of $K_{k}^{pd_m}$ and $K_{k}^{dm}$, it can be found that:
\begin{equation}\label{gd}
\begin{split}
&\mathbb{E}\{(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)AM_{k-1}^{m}A^\top(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)^\top\}\\
 &=\mathbb{E}\{((\tilde{K}_{k}^{pd_m}+I)(\tilde{K}_{k}^{dm}+I)-I)AM_{k-1}^{m}\\
 &\quad\times A^\top((\tilde{K}_{k}^{pd_m}+I)(\tilde{K}_{k}^{dm}+I)-I)^\top\} .
\end{split}
\end{equation}
Since $\tilde{K}_{k}^{pd_m}$ and $\tilde{K}_{k}^{dm}$ are diagonal random matrices with covariance matrices $\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pd_m}$ and $\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{dm}$, apply Lemma \ref{hada} to \eqref{gd} and obtain
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
&\mathbb{E}\{(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)A\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m}\hat{\eta}_{k-1}^{m\top} A^\top(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)^\top\}\\
 &=(\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pd_m}+\mathbf{1})\circ(\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{dm}+\mathbf{1})\circ(AM_{k-1}^{m}A^\top)-AM_{k-1}^{m}A^\top
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Similarly, we get
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
&\mathbb{E}\{(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)Ax_{k-1}x_{k-1}^\top A^\top(K_{k}^{pd_m}K_{k}^{dm}-I)^\top\}\\
&=(\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pd_m}+\mathbf{1})\circ(\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{dm}+\mathbf{1})\circ(A\Sigma_{k-1}A^\top)-A\Sigma_{k-1}A^\top
\end{split}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
&\mathbb{E}\{K_{k}^{pd_m}\tilde{v}_{k}^{dm}\tilde{v}_{k}^{dm\top} K_{k}^{pd_m\top}\}=(\tilde{\mathscr{K}}_{k}^{pd_m}+\mathbf{1})\circ\tilde{Q}_{k}^{dm}
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
According to the above equalities, the covariance $P_{k}^{pd_m}$ can be derived. At this point, the verification of all cases concerning the prediction error covariance $P_{k}^{pd_m,qd_n}$ is formally concluded. Along the similar line, the results of the other covariance matrices can also be established, thus completing the proof. 
\end{proof}

\subsection{MDP Formulation} \label{MDP}
% 要解决的实际问题->序列决策问题的数学表达式->依据什么决策（上一时刻）->可以建模为MDP->三元组的具体解释
The scheduler wishes to find a scheduling policy to minimize the average sum of the trace of the estimation error covariance matrices across all sensors and all times. We will formulate a MDP to solve the associated sequential decision making problem:
\begin{equation} \label{ACP}
    \min_{\{(a^{1}_{k},...,a^{M}_{k})\}}\limsup_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{k=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathrm{tr}M^{i}_{k}\right] \text{.}
\end{equation}
At time $k$, the scheduler makes the decisions based on the estimation error covariance of each sensor at the previous time step, i.e., $\{P_{1,k-1},$ $\cdots, P_{N,k-1}\}$, which is fully known to the scheduler. Therefore, this sequential decision making problem can be formulated as an MDP with average cost over the infinite time horizon in a triple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{C})$. Each element of the triple is defined as follows:

%yaoyao的信息scheduler
% 先说状态是什么，再说状态空间是怎么样的
\begin{itemize}
    \item {\it State space}: The MDP state is defined as the set $s_{k}=\{\tr(M_{k-1}^{1,1}),\cdots,\tr (M_{k-1}^{i,j}),\cdots,\tr (M_{k-1}^{N,N})\}$, encompassing traces of prior-step estimation error covariances and cross estimation error covariances. Thus, the state space is $\mathcal{S}=\mathbb{S}_{+}^{n_x}\times\cdots\times\mathbb{S}_{+}^{n_x}$, where $\mathbb{S}_{+}^n$ represents the set of $n$-dimensional positive definite and positive semi-definite matrix.
    % 先说动作是什么，再说动作空间是怎么样的（一共有几种动作）
    \item { \it Action space}: A generic element of action space is $a_k=(a^{1}_{k},\cdots,a^{M}_{k})$, corresponding to the $\frac{(\sum_{i=1}^N|\mathcal{N}_i|)!}{(\sum_{i=1}^N|\mathcal{N}_i|-M)!}$ different ways of allocating $M$ relays to $N$ sensors, that is, $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite space.
    \item {\it Cost function} $\mathcal{C}(s_{k},a_{k})$: The one-step cost $\mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$, incurred when taking action $a_k$ at state $s_k$, is defined as the summed trace of all sensors' estimation error covariance matrices, $\mathcal{C}(s_{k},a_{k})=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\tr(M_{i,k})$.
    
\end{itemize}






% MDP问题的目标->数学表达式
The goal of MDP is to find an optimal policy $\pi^*$ to minimize the expected average cost over the infinite time horizon:
\begin{equation*}
    \pi^*=\arg\min_{\pi\in\Pi}\mathcal{J}(\pi,s_0)
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{J}(\pi,s_0)\triangleq\limsup_{T\to\infty}\frac1T\sum_{k=0}^{T-1}\mathbb{E}_\pi[\mathcal{C}(s_k,a_k)|s_0]
\end{equation*}
for any initial state $s_{0}=\{M_{0}^{1},\cdots, M_{0}^{N}\}$.

% \end{equation*}

\subsection{Solving the Sensor Scheduling Problem Using Deep Reinforcement Learning}
As mentioned in Section \ref{MDP}, the scheduler's objective is to find a scheduling policy that minimizes the average sum of the trace of all sensors' estimation error covariance matrices, resolving the associated expected average cost problem. In this subsection, we will use reinforcement learning methods to approximate the solution of the expected average cost problem \eqref{ACP} by solving the associated discounted cost problem as follows:
\begin{equation} \label{DCP1}
\begin{split}
\min_{{\{(a_{1,k},...,a_{M,k})\}}}\limsup_{T\to\infty}\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{k=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\delta^{k}\mathrm{tr}P_{i,k}\right]
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\delta<1$ is a discount factor. In \cite{hernandez2012discrete}, it is shown that the scheduling policy found by solving the associated discounted cost problem with a discount factor $\delta$ close to $1$ is an $\epsilon$-optimal policy for the average cost problem. 

Problem \eqref{DCP1} is equivalent to the discounted reward maximization problem:
\begin{equation} \label{DCP2}
\begin{split}
\max_{{\{(a_{1,k},...,a_{M,k})\}}}\liminf_{T\to\infty}\mathbb{E}\left[-\sum_{k=0}^{T-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\delta^{k}\mathrm{tr}P_{i,k}\right]\text{.}
\end{split}
\end{equation}
In reinforcement learning, the Q-function (also known as the action-value function) is commonly used to represent the expected cumulative reward for a given state-action pair. The Q-function $Q(s,a)$ is typically defined as the expected future total reward by taking an action $a$ at state $s$. Following an optimal policy, the Q-function version of the Bellman equation for problem \eqref{DCP2} is:
\begin{equation}
Q^*(s,a)=\mathbb{E}\left[r+\delta\max_{a'}Q^*(s',a')\Big| s,a\right]
\end{equation}
where $s'$ represents next state given the current state $s$ and action $a$, and $Q^*(\cdot,\cdot)$ is the optimal Q-function.

Q-learning is a classic method in reinforcement learning that that determines an optimal policy by learning the Q-values associated with state-action pairs. This process enables the system to make optimal decisions based on its understanding of the situation. However, Q-learning is only suitable for small-scale state-action spaces, as the maintenance of the Q-value table becomes impractical as the state-action space grows. In CPS, the large scale of sensors makes it difficult to apply traditional Q-learning to solve scheduling problems due to high memory requirements and the challenge of meeting real-time processing demands.

To address this challenge, function approximation is employed to estimate the Q-function, particularly through the use of deep neural networks. This approach culminates in the development of DQN, which significantly enhance the learning process by efficiently approximating Q-values within high-dimensional state spaces. DQN demonstrates exceptional capability in handling large-scale, complex environments. Building upon DQN, some crucial innovations also have been proposed, including experience replay and target network updates. Experience replay involves storing the agent's interactions and sampling training data randomly, thereby breaking the correlation between consecutive experiences and improving the stability of the training process. Target network updates periodically update the target Q-network, mitigating excessive oscillations in Q-value estimates and further stabilizing the learning process.

Based on the above concepts, we present our approach for solving problem \eqref{DCP2} in Algorithm \ref{MDP-algorithm}. 
The state $s_k$ is chosen as
\begin{equation*}
s_{k}=\{\tr(M_{k-1}^{1,1}),\cdots,\tr (M_{k-1}^{i,j}),\cdots,\tr (M_{k-1}^{N,N})\}.
\end{equation*}
The action is set as
\begin{equation*}
a_k=(a^{1}_{k},\cdots,a^{M}_{k}) \text{,}
\end{equation*}
corresponds to the allocation of the $M$ relays at time $k$, and the single stage reward is given by
\begin{equation*}
r_k=\sum_{i=1}^{N}-\tr(P_{i,k}) .
\end{equation*}
The information of the hyper-parameters used in Algorithm \ref{MDP-algorithm} can be referred to Section \ref{sec:experiment}.

\begin{algorithm}\label{MDP-algorithm}
\caption{DQN for Distributed Wireless Relay Allocation}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Initialize:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Replay buffer $\mathcal{P}$ with capacity $K$
        \item Online network $Q$ with random weights $\theta_0$
        \item Target network $\hat{Q}$ with weights $\theta^- = \theta_0$
        \item Initial state $s_0$
    \end{itemize}
\FOR{time step $k = 0, 1, \dots, T$}
    \STATE Select action according to the $\epsilon$-greedy policy:
        \[
        a_k \gets \begin{cases} 
            \text{random action} & \text{with probability $\epsilon$}  \\
            \arg\max_a Q(s_k, a; \theta) & \text{otherwise}
        \end{cases}
        \]
    \STATE Perform $a_k$ to obtain reward $r_k$ and observe next\\ state $s_{k+1}$
    \STATE Store transition $(s_k, a_k, r_k, s_{k+1})$ in $\mathcal{P}$
    \STATE Sample random mini-batch of transitions $(s_j, a_j, r_j, s_{j+1})$ from $\mathcal{P}$
    \STATE Compute target: $z_j = r_j + \delta \max_{a'} \hat{Q}(s_{j+1}, a'; \theta^-)$ \\for each sample in mini-batch
    \STATE Execute a mini-batch gradient descent step with loss $(z_j - Q(s_j, a_j; \theta_k))^2$ to obtain $\theta_{k+1}$
    \STATE every $c$ steps set $\theta^- = \theta_k$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
\begin{remark}

We know that the deep neural network in Algorithm \ref{MDP-algorithm} needs to be fed with a vector. Drawing inspiration from convolutional neural network architectures for image processing, we modify the state vector by using matrix trace operations, i.e., $s_{k}=\{\tr(M_{k-1}^{1,1}),\cdots,\tr (M_{k-1}^{i,j}),\cdots,\tr (M_{k-1}^{N,N})\}$, which will accelerate the convergence of the algorithm \cite{}. Furthermore, except incorporating each sensor's local estimation error covariance in the state vector, we augment the cross estimation error covariance matrices between each sensor and its neighbors, which we have found could also help giving faster convergence for the algorithm in some cases.
%此外，我们除了使用各传感器的估计误差协方差的迹作为状态向量，我们还增加了各传感器和邻居之间的估计误差协方差（cross estimation covariance），这也是为了加速算法的收敛，因为这样可以增加状态向量的信息量。
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The efficiency of the algorithm is tested on 2 relays and 6 sensors in the subsequent section, which is a
consideration of hardware restriction and training time. It is certainly scalable to extend the approach to a significantly larger amount of systems and sensors, provided with sufficient computational capacity.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion} \label{sec:conclusion} 
This paper has investigated the relay allocation problem for distributed state estimation in relay-assisted sensor networks, where only a subset of sensors can transmit data through relays at each time step due to resource constraints. An optimal distributed filtering algorithm has been developed using the Kalman consensus filtering logic to process local estimates affected by fading channels and relay transmissions. Relays are dynamically assigned to sensors to optimize the estimation performance of the whole system. The assignment problem has been formulated as an MDP and addressed using a DQN-based approach, enabling scalability to larger sensor networks compared to traditional methods. Numerical examples have been illustrated to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed algorithm. Future research could extend the proposed scheme to scenarios involving packet losses and asynchronous communication.
